Accessibility in The Subway Still A Concern

by: Caithlin Peña

The Roosevelt Ave. stop at Jackson Heights is one of the MTA’s accessible stations in Queens but the elevators aren’t always reliable.

“Just as we were coming up the steps, there was no elevator working,” says Danna Dennis, senior organizer for the Riders Alliance. “I was completely out of breath.”

Riders Alliance, a non-profit organization that fights for a more reliable public transit in New York, are advocating for more funding for New York’s subway that would provide more accessibility for riders, which means more elevators and a more updated signal system.

Jackson Heights-Roosevelt Ave. is just one of 21 accessible stations in Queens out of 81. And out of 472 stations in New York City, only 135 are accessible.

This particular stop houses the 7, E, F, M, and R trains. And the surrounding the area has several bus stops, including the Q47 and Q70 that go to LaGuardia Airport and the Q32 that goes to Penn Station in Midtown.

Since it’s such a busy area with many commuters, this makes the need for working elevators more important than ever.

In 2018, 84% of subway stations reported at least a week’s worth of elevator outages, according to a Daily News report.

Suraj Jaswal, a Libertarian Party candidate for the City Council from District 25, which includes Elmhurst, Jackson Heights, and part of Woodside, supports the bill and accessibility in the subways is one of his concerns. He believes that it’s not up to standards.

“My own friends who are disabled complain regularly,” he said during a Riders Alliance rally.

Riders Alliance is also advocating for the Stronger Communities through Better Transit Act, which has been referred to the Subcommittee on Highways and Transit. The bill, if passed, will provide $3 billion in annual funding for subways and buses, as well as an additional $20 billion in capital funding to fix the subway and make it more accessible to commuters. The additional funding will also help avoid fare hikes and cuts to transit workers.

Accessible stations, according to the MTA website, are also equipped with an AutoGate, a gate that allows passengers with ambulatory issues, accompanied by a service animal or uses a mobility device to access the subway.

The MTA's P.A. system now includes announcements on whether a subway stop is accessible or not and where it is located at the station. Riders can also check the status of elevators and escalators on the MTA’s website at specific subway stops.
